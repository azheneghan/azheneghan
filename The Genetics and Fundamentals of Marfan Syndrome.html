The Genetics and Fundamentals of Marfan Syndrome

 

Andrew Heneghan

 

May 11, 2022

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Abstract

Marfan syndrome is a rare genetic condition that weaken the functions of the connective tissue inside the body. The main cause are one or more pathogenic variants in the FBN1 or fibrillin protein gene. Important cellular pathways affected by these variants include the Integrin Pathway, the Phospholipase-C Pathway, and the intracellular TGFβ signaling pathway. Change in TGFβ signaling activation is the main element of pathogenesis in Marfan syndrome. Marfan syndrome is diagnosed with defined clinical criteria, such as Ghent nosology, and is associated with many symptoms including lesions, such as bone damage, aortic dissection, and similar kinds of damage, in the skeletal and cardiovascular systems. This literature review will summarize the genetics, pathology, and biomedical ethics of Marfan syndrome through examination of different studies related to these topics.

 

Introduction

Marfan syndrome is a hereditary connective tissue disorder that is caused by pathogenic variants in the FBN1 gene. It follows an autosomal dominant mode of inheritance and has an estimated prevalence of one in every 10,000 individuals. In Marfan syndrome the bodys connective tissue is weakened, causing extended extremities that affect many body parts (Yuan and Jing 2010). The average life expectancy of patients with Marfan syndrome is 70 years. Diagnosis is usually done in late childhood years when symptoms and other signs start to show. More severe cases of Marfan syndrome, including neonatal Marfan syndrome, can appear in the early years of an infant and develop very quickly in the childhood phase. Marfan syndrome is detected using blood tests and is mainly diagnosed with revised Ghent nosology, involving major and minor diagnostic findings or an echocardiogram. Doctors may also check a patient for Marfan syndrome if he or she shows symptoms or a family history. Life expectancy can actually be a lot shorter if family history is present (Yuan and Jing 2010).

 

The more common symptoms of Marfan syndrome are weakened tissue in the skeletal and cardiovascular systems, very flexible joints, unusually long limbs, the breastbone sinking into or sticking out of the chest, and scoliosis. Individuals with Marfan syndrome are also at higher risk of eye problems, including nearsightedness and cataracts. Most of all, cardiovascular malformations, such as heart valve diseases like mitral valve prolapse and thoracic aortic aneurysm, are the most life-threatening symptoms. Strategies for managing Marfan syndrome should be blood pressure control and restrictions on physical activities, usually under integrated care from a team of specialists (Yuan and Jing 2010). As for thoracic aortic aneurysm, which results from Marfan syndrome, surgical intervention is the only known treatment (de la Fuente-Alonso A et al. 2021).

 

Genetics

Marfan syndrome mainly involves different pathogenic variants, whether it be defects or deletions, of the fibrillin-1 (FBN1) gene, resulting in a loss of function mutation reducing Fibrillin protein levels or creating proteins that work differently. Regardless of the type of variant, the result is a drastic weakening of the connective tissue. Other variants at places where cryptic splicing occurs give PTC resulting in similar kinds of decreased Fibrillin levels (Arnaud and Milleron, et al. 2021). FBN1 seems to be the only gene in which different variants directly cause Marfan syndrome. A linkage study of a Marfan-like disorder in a large family did not prove that disorder was actually linked to Marfan syndrome or the Fib15 and FBN1 loci, where this genetic disease originates (Boileau et al. 1993). Studies have suggested the location of the chromosome 11 locus and that there are two additional loci modifiers. One modifier is the gMod-M2 on chromosome 10, which contains the PRKG1 gene. Variants in this gene tend to lead to gain-of-function mutations that trigger thoracic aortic aneurysm. The other modifier is gModM3 on chromosome 11, which contains the SLN gene. This gene creates sarcolipin protein, which is weakly expressed in the aortic wall and fibroblasts for the skin (Aubart et al. 2018).

 

Some cases of Marfans syndrome result in mosaic expression, where a single individual has two separate genotypes. New sequencing capture technology also allowed researchers to find five MFS proband cases who had a FBN1 gene mosaic pathogenic variant. These probands did display some symptoms and signs of Marfan syndrome (Arnaud and Morel et al. 2021). Most of the severe cases are due to sporadic mutation in one parents single germ cell (Yuan and Jing 2010).

 

Marfan syndrome and its autosomal dominant inheritance pattern is characterized by high clinical variability along with isolated early onset familial weakening of aorta in the lower parts of the body (Overwater et al. 2019). Beta-blocker therapy, in which a group of medications is used to control heart and heart valve problems, appeared to show an underlined risk of global aortic dissection (Milleron et al. 2020). Pregnant women with Marfan syndrome also seem to be able to have acute aortic dissection, especially when they have aortic root dilation. They can also have acute aortic dissection in cases of disease inheritance by the child. Fast-paced aortic dilation and aortic dissection may occur during or after the pregnancy at any time, but it happens in the third trimester most of the time (Yuan and Jing 2010).

 

Pathology

Most of the pathogenic variants that cause Marfan syndrome come from FBN1, a gene that produces fibrillin-1, an extracellular protein important for forming strong connective tissue between cells. Connective tissue is composed of a matrix of living cells, especially fibroblast, an organic protein or substance, and an inorganic substance like water. FBN1 with pathogenic variants affect the protein, causing defects in the assembly of the connective tissue, mainly in the fibroblast cells that make fibers for connecting the tissue. The main element of pathogenesis in Marfan syndrome is irregular activation of TGFβ signaling, along with intracellular TGFβ signaling pathway activation, which plays an vital role in the formation of thoracic aortic aneurysms (Sakai et al. 2016). When it comes to the FBN1 gene, the presence of ADAMTSL4 with an unaffected FBN1 gene leads to the TGFβ pathway being inactive. When the FBN1 gene is altered, ADAMTSL4 unnaturally activates the TGFβ pathway, resulting in Marfan syndrome and weaker connective tissue (Figure 1, Gustafson and Bjork 2022).

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 


Most studies taken place in these sources are clinical trials involving human subjects, making the data directly relevant to humans, but other studies use mice with the FBN1 gene variant that determines Marfan syndrome to study effects on their pathways. One study by the Taiwan Mouse Clinic used Fbn1C1039G/+ mice to examine whether thoracic aortic aneurysm development is affected by great levels of homocysteine in the bloodstream. After going through many experiment phases, the results were revealed to be statistically significant. Vitamin B put expression of these genes back to their natural levels and gave a way to lessen thoracic aortic dilation (Huang et al. 2021). Fbn1C1039G/+ mice are used in several experiments related to Marfan syndrome when it comes to investigating the pathology of Marfan syndrome. Another study that was done to look at the IL11 and its role in the aorta of Marfan syndrome by crossing the mMFS mice to IL11ra1-/- mice. It appeared that deleting IL11ra1, with the use of anti-IL11ra1, actually reduces the chances of getting a thoracic aortic aneurysm (Lim et al. 2022). These studies seem to show that micronutrients like vitamin B and deleting certain genomes with gene therapy do appear to lower the risk of development of thoracic aortic aneurysm.

 

Biomedical Ethics

Most of these studies, consisting of children and/or adults suspected of having Marfan syndrome, need to follow the principles of biomedical ethics, with most of the emphasis being on patient autonomy and informed consent. For instance, 19 adolescents with Marfan syndrome were questioned at the Amsterdam University Medical Centers, and the Medical Ethics Review Committee waived ethical approval since there was voluntary participation and written informed consent from all the subjects (Warnink-Kavelaars et al. 2019). It is hard to believe that waiving ethical approval was the best idea in this case because it makes one wonder if there is some detail not mentioned that breaks a principal of biomedical ethics. These children may not have known what they consented to. The same can be said for two other studies. One was with the French National Reference Centre in Paris for somatic mosaicism in relation to the FBN1 gene. Both ethical approval and informed consent were attained, but these were said to be required of French bioethics laws (Arnaud P and Morel H et al. 2021). The other was in Hungary where 45 Marfan patients had their states of anxiety tested after having one of three kinds of cardiac surgery. Written informed consent and ethical approval from the Scientific and Research Ethical Committee was also obtained from these patients, like the previous studies. These patients, however, were more thoroughly educated about the study and all its details and protocols for data safety (Benke K et al. 2017). Obtaining informed consent seems to exist throughout the studies, but the amount of patient education seems to vary across studies.

 

Conclusion and Insights

Many questions still remain surrounding Marfan syndrome, including those related to cardiovascular issues or neonatal Marfan syndrome. The main goal surrounding these different FBN1 gene pathogenic variants is to study their influence on how Marfan syndrome is expressed in those affected. One outstanding gap is little research that is focused on curing this disorder. Gene therapy could possibly be an effective means of dealing with Marfan syndrome. Such technologies for editing the genome have worked before for other kinds of gene therapy and can repair the pathogenic variants of the FBN1 gene as well. Unfortunately, researchers and Marfan syndrome groups mainly just help people try and live with Marfan syndrome instead of looking for a more permanent treatment. Then again, this is a genetic disease, so preventing it is not easy. Perhaps the great number of FBN1 variants means lots more clinical studies and tests must be run before a cure can be found.
